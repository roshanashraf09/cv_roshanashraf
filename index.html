Origin

Born from the Ottoman Empire, beloved by the world.

Shawarma traces its roots to the döner kebab of 19th-century Ottoman Turkey — meat stacked on a vertical spit, slowly turning beside a flame. As the dish travelled through the Levant, it transformed into something distinctly its own: marinated layers of lamb, chicken, or beef, perfumed with cumin, cinnamon, and cardamom.

Today it feeds millions daily — from Beirut streetside carts to London wrap shops — carrying with it the flavour of a culture that never tired of feeding strangers well.

Craft

Building the spit is itself an art.

Each layer of meat is seasoned differently. Fat is interspersed throughout to baste the layers below as the spit turns. The outer surface chars and crisps; the shawarma master shaves it tableside in long, rhythmic strokes with a broad knife.

The bread — whether thin markouk, fluffy pita, or rolled flatbread — is warmed directly on the grill. Then come the pickles, the toum, the tahini, the parsley. Nothing is rushed. Nothing is optional.

Regional Variations

One dish, many accents.

Lebanese — Wrapped in markouk with toum (garlic cream), pickles, and tomatoes. Often chicken, always generous.

Turkish döner — The ancestor. Served in a dürüm roll or over rice with yogurt and chilli butter.

Iraqi — Heavier spicing, served in a bulkier bread pocket with potato crisps and sumac onions.

Egyptian — Often beef or kofta, served in a toasted bun with tahini and a vinegary salad.

Gulf-style — Milder spicing, more garlic sauce, sometimes with chips tucked inside the wrap.
